EMI Explained: The Math Your Bank Doesn't Show You

📖 12 min read 📅 27 April 2026 ✍ By Ajit Kumar

In year 1 of a 20-year home loan, only 16% of your EMI reduces principal. The other 84% is interest. By year 15, that flips. Understanding this — and the simple actions it implies — is the difference between paying ₹54 lakh in interest and ₹40 lakh.

TL;DR

The four EMI truths

1. EMI stays constant. Principal/interest split changes drastically over time.

2. Early-year prepayments save 5-8x more than late-year prepayments.

3. When prepaying, ask the bank to reduce tenure, not EMI. Saves 2-3x more.

4. Foreclosure is FREE on floating-rate home loans. RBI banned charges in 2014.

The formula

Every EMI calculation in India uses the same formula. Banks dress it up with calculators, sliders, and animations, but the math is identical:

EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1)

Where:

  • P = Principal (loan amount)
  • r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12 / 100)
  • n = Total number of monthly instalments

For a ₹50,00,000 loan at 8.5% over 20 years:

  • P = 50,00,000
  • r = 8.5 / 12 / 100 = 0.00708
  • n = 20 × 12 = 240 months
  • EMI = ₹43,391/month

Multiply that by 240 and you've paid ₹1,04,13,898 over the life of the loan. ₹50 lakh of that was your principal. The other ₹54.13 lakh was interest. You paid back more than double what you borrowed. This is the reality of a 20-year loan that no one says out loud at the time you sign.

What changes over the life of the loan

Here's what your bank's app doesn't show you in any meaningful way: the split of every monthly EMI between principal repayment and interest payment.

Notice that in Year 1, every ₹43,391 EMI only reduces your principal by ₹7,055. The rest is interest. This is why outstanding balance barely moves in early years.

The reason for this asymmetry is interest is calculated on the outstanding balance every month. When the balance is ₹50 lakh (year 1), the monthly interest at 8.5% is roughly ₹35,000. Your EMI is ₹43,391. So only ~₹8,000 reduces principal. The next month, balance is ₹49.92 lakh — interest is slightly less, principal repayment is slightly more. Compounding works in slow motion.

By year 15, the outstanding has fallen to ~₹28 lakh. Monthly interest is ~₹20,000 instead of ₹35,000. The remaining ₹23,000 of your EMI reduces principal aggressively. The loan is finally getting paid down.

The prepayment insight

Now combine the table above with one observation: any extra rupee you pay reduces the outstanding balance immediately. From the next month onwards, your interest is calculated on a smaller balance — so a larger portion of your EMI goes to principal — so the balance falls faster — and so on.

This compounds dramatically over time. ₹1 lakh prepaid early acts on a high-interest balance for many years. ₹1 lakh prepaid late acts on a small balance for very few years.

💡 Action

Don't wait for "a comfortable amount" to start prepaying. Every spare rupee in years 1–7 is worth 5–8x more than the same rupee in years 14–20. Even a ₹10,000 prepayment in year 2 saves ~₹35,000 in lifetime interest.

EMI vs Tenure: the silent question

When you make a partial prepayment, your bank usually asks (or just defaults): "Would you like to reduce EMI or reduce tenure?" Most banks default to reducing EMI because it feels good — your monthly burden drops.

This is mathematically the worse choice. Here's the comparison on a ₹50L / 8.5% / 20-year loan, after prepaying ₹5 lakh at the end of year 5:

Figure 3: Reduce EMI vs Reduce Tenure (₹5L prepaid in year 5)
StrategyNew EMINew TenureTotal Interest Saved
No prepayment₹43,39120 yrsbaseline

Same ₹5 lakh prepayment, very different outcomes. Reducing tenure saves you ₹14 lakh — more than 2x the savings from reducing EMI.

Why the difference? When you reduce tenure, you keep paying the higher EMI for fewer years — so the loan closes faster, and you stop paying interest sooner. When you reduce EMI, you keep paying for the same 20 years, so the bank gets to charge interest for the full original tenure — just on a slightly smaller balance.

⚠ At every prepayment

Tell the bank, in writing: "Apply this prepayment to reduce tenure, not EMI." Get the revised amortization schedule. If they don't volunteer this option, ask. They are obligated to provide it.

Three other things they don't tell you

Foreclosure is FREE on floating-rate loans

RBI banned all foreclosure charges and prepayment penalties on floating-rate home loans in 2014. Your bank cannot legally charge you anything to close your loan early or make partial prepayments. Some banks still try — through "documentation fees" or "processing charges." These are not legal. If you encounter resistance, ask them to show you the RBI circular permitting the charge. They cannot, because none exists.

Note: this applies only to floating-rate retail loans. Fixed-rate loans can have foreclosure charges (typically 2-4%), and corporate loans have separate rules.

Tax benefits cap your effective rate

Under Section 24(b), interest paid on a home loan is deductible up to ₹2,00,000/year for a self-occupied property. Section 80C allows up to ₹1,50,000/year deduction for principal repayment. For a borrower in the 30% tax bracket, this saves ₹60,000 + ₹45,000 = ₹1,05,000/year in taxes.

Effectively, this lowers the real cost of your loan. On a ₹50 lakh loan, your stated 8.5% rate effectively becomes ~7.4% after tax savings (assuming you fully use the deductions). This doesn't mean don't prepay — it means home loans are not as expensive as they look.

Float-rate loans don't reset monthly

Even on EBLR (External Benchmark Lending Rate) loans where rates auto-adjust to RBI repo, the reset is typically every 3 months — not monthly. On older MCLR loans, reset is every 6 or 12 months. So when RBI cuts the rate, don't expect your EMI to drop next month. Read your sanction letter — find your reset frequency. If you don't know what yours is, call the bank today.

What to do, concretely

  1. This week: Pull out your loan sanction letter. Find your interest rate, MCLR/EBLR type, reset date, and current outstanding balance.
  2. This month: Run the EMI calculator with your real numbers. See how much you've actually paid in interest vs principal so far. It's usually shocking.
  3. If on MCLR: Request conversion to EBLR. The conversion fee (₹5-15K) is recovered in months. Saves ₹2-5L over remaining tenure typically.
  4. Set up systematic prepayment: Even ₹5,000/month extra over the loan saves lakhs. Make it automatic — same day as your salary.
  5. On every windfall (bonus, FD maturity, gift): prepay. Specify "reduce tenure, not EMI."
  6. Track: Use the EMI Quick Reference PDF. Print it. Stick it on your wall.
📌 The 1-page summary

EMI = constant amount, but split changes wildly over time. Years 1-7: prepay aggressively. Always reduce tenure, not EMI. Foreclosure is FREE. Convert MCLR to EBLR. Do these four things, and over a 20-year loan, you'll save ₹10-15 lakh compared to a borrower who does none of them. That's a serious return for 30 minutes of attention.
